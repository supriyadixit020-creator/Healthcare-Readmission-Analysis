TOP 5 BUSINESS INSIGHTS & RECOMMENDATIONS
================================================================

INSIGHT 1 — Nearly Half of All Patients Are Readmitted
----------------------------------------------------------------
Finding:
Overall readmission rate is 46.31% — meaning nearly 1 in 2 diabetic
patients returns to hospital after discharge.
Breakdown:
  - NO readmission:    53.69% (53,818 patients)
  - Readmitted >30d:   35.09% (35,173 patients)
  - Readmitted <30d:   11.22% (11,250 patients)

The <30-day readmission group is particularly concerning — it
signals that patients were discharged before being fully stable.

Root Cause:
Premature discharge, inadequate post-discharge care planning,
and uncontrolled blood glucose management are the primary drivers.

Recommendation:
- Implement a structured 30-day post-discharge follow-up program
  for ALL diabetic patients before discharge
- Flag high-risk patients (using the risk scoring model in this
  analysis) for extended inpatient monitoring before discharge
- Establish automated 7-day and 14-day check-in calls for
  recently discharged patients

Expected Impact:
Reducing <30-day readmissions by even 15% could prevent ~1,687
unnecessary readmissions annually, significantly reducing costs.

================================================================

INSIGHT 2 — Emergency Admissions Are the Highest-Risk Entry Point
----------------------------------------------------------------
Finding:
Emergency admissions have a readmission rate of 47.49%, the
highest among all structured admission types — nearly 7 points
higher than Elective admissions (40.99%).

Emergency accounts for the largest patient volume (27,958 patients
= 27.9% of all encounters), making this the single highest-impact
segment to target.

Root Cause:
Emergency patients arrive in acute/unstable condition, often with
unmanaged glucose levels. The urgency of care leaves limited time
for proper discharge planning and patient education.

Recommendation:
- Deploy a dedicated "Diabetic Emergency Care Team" for inpatient
  stabilization of emergency admissions
- Require a mandatory diabetes care education session before
  discharge for all emergency diabetic patients
- Partner with community health workers for post-emergency
  home visits within 72 hours of discharge

Expected Impact:
Reducing emergency readmission rate from 47.49% to 42% could
save approximately 1,400 readmissions from this segment alone.

================================================================

INSIGHT 3 — Elderly Patients (70-90) Face the Highest Readmission Risk
----------------------------------------------------------------
Finding:
Age groups [70-80) and [80-90) show the highest readmission rates:
  - [80-90): 48.26% readmission rate
  - [70-80): 48.21% readmission rate
  - [60-70): 46.38% readmission rate

Together, these three age groups represent 65,748 patients (65.6%
of total encounters) — the dominant patient population.

Root Cause:
Elderly diabetic patients often have multiple comorbidities,
reduced mobility, and limited ability to self-manage post-discharge
care. Social isolation and caregiver gaps amplify the risk.

Recommendation:
- Create age-specific discharge protocols for patients 70+
- Involve family members/caregivers in discharge planning sessions
- Prioritize elderly patients for community nursing visit programs
- Simplify medication regimens where possible to improve adherence

Expected Impact:
Targeted elderly care programs can reduce their readmission rates
by an estimated 5-8 percentage points.

================================================================

INSIGHT 4 — High Visit History = Strongest Readmission Predictor
----------------------------------------------------------------
Finding:
Total prior visits (outpatient + emergency + inpatient combined)
showed the STRONGEST correlation with readmission (r = 0.205)
of all features analyzed — higher than medications, diagnoses,
age, or hospital stay duration.

Visit category readmission rates:
  - High Utilization (5+ visits):   significantly elevated
  - Moderate Utilization (3-4):      elevated
  - Low Utilization (1-2):           moderate
  - No Previous Visits:              lowest risk

Root Cause:
Patients with high prior healthcare utilization have more complex,
chronic conditions that repeatedly destabilize — they are already
"known" high-risk patients in the system.

Recommendation:
- Create a "High Utilizer" watchlist for patients with 5+ prior visits
- Assign a dedicated care coordinator to high-utilizer patients
- Proactively schedule these patients for regular diabetes management
  clinic visits to avoid emergency escalation
- Review medication regimens quarterly for high-utilizer patients

Expected Impact:
Proactive management of the top 10% highest utilizers (4,389
patients) could prevent a disproportionate share of readmissions.

================================================================

INSIGHT 5 — Insulin Dosage Changes Signal Instability
----------------------------------------------------------------
Finding:
Patients whose insulin was adjusted ("Down" or "Up") show higher
readmission rates than stable patients:
  - Insulin "Down":   52.88% readmission rate (HIGHEST)
  - Insulin "Up":     51.68%
  - Insulin "Steady": 45.42%
  - No insulin:       43.92%

Root Cause:
Insulin dose changes indicate active glucose management challenges.
"Down" adjustments may signal hypoglycemic episodes — a serious
complication that increases readmission risk.

Recommendation:
- Flag all patients with insulin dose changes (Up or Down) as
  high-priority for extended monitoring before discharge
- Implement a glucose stabilization protocol: patients with insulin
  adjustments should not be discharged until glucose readings are
  stable for 24+ hours
- Provide targeted insulin self-management education for patients
  with recent dose changes

Expected Impact:
Stabilizing glucose before discharge could reduce insulin-change
patients' readmission rate from ~52% toward the "Steady" baseline
of 45%, preventing ~700 readmissions annually.
